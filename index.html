Light Rail

A City Decides to Connect

In 1996, King County voters approved Sound Transit's Regional Transit Authority plan. Five years later, the first shovel broke ground on what would become one of the fastest-growing light rail systems in the United States — Link Light Rail.

Today the system spans over 36 stations and 116 miles, stretching from Everett in the north to Tacoma and Redmond. What began as a modest urban connector has become the spine of a regional transformation.

Phase 1

Central Link Opens

On July 18, 2009, Central Link opened between Westlake Station in downtown Seattle and Tukwila International Boulevard — 15.6 miles of initial service. Within months, an extension to Sea-Tac Airport followed, connecting the city to its international gateway.

Ridership exceeded initial forecasts, validating the region's appetite for rail. The airport connection alone shifted how residents and visitors moved through the south end.

Transit-Oriented Dev

The TOD Awakening

Within walking distance of Link stations, a new kind of development calculus emerged. Developers, municipalities, and non-profits began racing to rezone, build mixed-use projects, and create walkable nodes anchored by transit access.

Columbia City, Beacon Hill, and Rainier Avenue corridors saw the earliest surges — neighborhoods with existing community fabric that rail access turbocharged into denser, more economically active places.

Northgate Extension

North Seattle Opens Up

The Northgate Link Extension opened October 2, 2021, adding U District, Roosevelt, and Northgate stations. Overnight, the University of Washington to Northgate trip dropped from a 45-minute bus ride to an 8-minute train ride.

The Roosevelt and U District station areas saw some of Seattle's most aggressive upzoning — six-story mixed-use minimum heights within the Urban Village boundaries, catalyzing hundreds of new residential units.

2 Line — East Link

Crossing the Lake

The 2 Line (East Link) opened in April 2024, crossing Lake Washington on a pontoon bridge to serve Bellevue, Bel-Red, and Redmond — linking Seattle's urban core directly to the Eastside tech corridor for the first time.

On the Seattle side, the 2 Line shares the International District/Chinatown station with the 1 Line, reinforcing one of the city's most transit-dense nodes. The Rainier Valley and Central District neighborhoods visible here have seen sustained multifamily construction pressure as both lines converge nearby.

Note: The housing permit data in this project covers Seattle city limits only. Eastside TOD patterns in Bellevue and Redmond are not represented and would require a separate dataset.

Housing

Building Near the Tracks

Seattle's Mandatory Housing Affordability (MHA) program, enacted in 2019 following the HALA recommendations, requires developers building in upzoned areas to either include affordable units on-site or pay into an affordable housing fund.

Station areas near Link have disproportionately benefited — and borne the pressure. Rainier Valley communities have pushed for anti-displacement protections as rising rents follow rail investment. The tension between access and affordability is the central housing story of Link's second decade.

Context & Caveats

Inflation accounts for a significant share of this increase. Adjusting for CPI, real rent growth is roughly half the nominal figures shown. The BLS CPI-U rose approximately 42% between 2009 and 2023 — meaning $1,000 in 2009 rent equates to ~$1,420 in 2023 dollars before any real increase. BLS Inflation Calculator ↗

High demand — not rail alone — drives rents. The Seattle metro added roughly 350,000 net new jobs between 2010 and 2019 (BLS Current Employment Statistics ↗), with Amazon alone employing ~50,000 people in Seattle by 2018. Over the same decade, Seattle's population grew 21% — adding over 128,000 residents — making it one of only 14 U.S. cities to grow by 100,000+ in a single census period (Seattle OPCD / 2020 Census ↗). Less than 30% of Seattle adults were born in Washington state, reflecting how much of this growth came from in-migration (ACS via Census ↗). Increased demand for housing near employment centers would have raised rents with or without transit investment.

New housing construction limits rent growth. Research consistently shows that adding multifamily supply moderates rent increases over time. A 2024 NYU Furman Center study found that increases in housing supply decrease rents or slow rent growth both regionally and in surrounding neighborhoods (Housing Policy Debate ↗). A Pew Charitable Trusts analysis found every 10% increase in housing supply correlated with rents growing 5% less (Pew Charitable Trusts ↗). A 2025 study in the Journal of Political Economy found a 1% increase in new supply lowers average rents by 0.19%, equally in high-demand markets (Journal of Political Economy Macroeconomics ↗). Without the thousands of units built near Link stations, rents in these neighborhoods would likely be higher still.

Walkable, transit-served neighborhoods are consistently more desirable — and priced accordingly. Brookings Institution research found that each step up the walkability ladder adds over $300/month to apartment rents on average, nationally (Walk Score / Brookings ↗). A study of nearly 1 million home sales across 18 metro areas found homes in highly walkable neighborhoods sold for 23.5% more than comparable car-dependent properties (Redfin Research ↗). Higher rents near Link stations are partly a reflection of what people are willing to pay for access — not solely a consequence of displacement.

What's Next

The System Completes

Extensions to Everett, Tacoma Dome, and Ballard–West Seattle are in design and construction phases. The Ballard extension, in particular, will pass through some of Seattle's densest, most transit-hungry neighborhoods — Interbay, Uptown, and Lower Queen Anne.

Each new segment brings new TOD pressure — and new opportunity. Cities along the spine are updating station area plans, upzoning transit corridors, and negotiating community benefit agreements to ensure growth is equitable.
